Law of large numbers(LLN)

Visualize Law of Large Numbers
Assume there is a random process and we managed to get a sample out of that process.
Say we got \(n\) observations of that random process \(X,X_1,X_2,X_3,....,X_n\).
Here every observation is a Random variable and all of them has same random process therefore they are identically distributed and all of them are independent of each other, so we call then \(i.i.d\)(Independent and identically distributed) random variables.
Let's take our Counting Balls example:  ,  .
Their we want to estimate the proportion of Red balls and Blue balls.
We have a population of \(5000\) balls and we took a sample of \(n=300\) balls and to find the proportion of red balls we took average of our sample.
Law of Large Numbers says as we increases our sample size \((n)\) our sample average goes toward the True average (this is what we referred as Truth in our central dogma )
So if we increases our sample size from \(n=300\) to say something like \(n=400\) then we should get a better estimate of the true mean.
Say that the True Average is \(\mu\).
So according to the Law of Large Numbers,
\[\overline{X}_n:=\frac{1}{n}\sum _{i=1}^ n X_ i \xrightarrow [n\to \infty ] {\mathbb{P},\text{ a.s.}} \mu\]

Note that \(\mu\) is a constant and \(\overline{X}_n\) is a Random variable
\(:=\quad\) this symbol means "by definition"
\(\mathbb{P}\quad\) it means "in Probability"
\(\text{a.s.}\quad\) it means "almost surely"(with probability \(1\))

Law of large numbers are of two types:
  1. Weak law of large numbers: \[\overline{X}_n:=\frac{1}{n}\sum _{i=1}^ n X_ i \xrightarrow [n\to \infty ]{\mathbb{P}} \mu\]
    Explanation
    For any \(c\geq0\), \[\mathbb{P}\left(|\overline{X}_n - \mu|\geq c\right) \xrightarrow [n\to \infty ]{} 0\]
    This is convergence in probability

    Now think a very small number, like \(0.00001\) now convergence in probability says that if \(n\) is large enough then it's highly unlikely for \(\overline{X}_n\) to be more than \(0.00001\) units away from \(\mu\).

    Or say that if \(n\) is large then it's extreamly likely that \(\overline{X}_n\) is extreamly close to \(\mu\)

  2. Strong law of large numbers: \[\overline{X}_n:=\frac{1}{n}\sum _{i=1}^ n X_ i \xrightarrow [n\to \infty ]{\text{ a.s.}} \mu\]
Note: \(\text{ a.s.}\) implies \(\mathbb{P}\)
Ok now we know that the Law of large numbers says if we have large enough Sample size then our estimator \(\overline{X}_n\) and real parameter \(\mu\) are close \(\overline{X}_n \xrightarrow [n\to \infty] {} \mu\), but how much close, we don't know!
We can think it as:
\[ \left|\overline{X}_n -\mu \right| \propto \frac{1}{f(n)} \] where \(f(n)\) is an increasing function w.r.t. \(n\).
As \(f(n)\) increases \(\left|\overline{X}_n -\mu\right| \) decreases, so we want a function \(f(n)\) that increases rapidly w.r.t. \(n\).
For example \(log(log(n))\) increases very slowly so function like it are no useful.
So what is the rate are which \(\overline{X}_n\) approaches \(\mu\)?
The answer is hidden in Central Limit Theorem.

Gambler's Fallacy

Gambler's Fallacy is a rather popular mistaken belief that,
If an independent event is occurring more frequently (then it normally does), then it's less likely to occur in the future.
Note that this statement is not true, as it's a mistaken belief
Example:
Say you start flipping a fair coin \((p=0.5)\), and you observe that first \(20\) tosses to be \(\text{Heads}\).
Then some might say that,
"According to Law of Large Numbers the average proportion of \(\text{Heads}\) shall be \(50\%\) and we got \(20\) \(\text{Heads}\) in a row so there are high chances for our next toss to be \(\text{Tails}\)."
But the above statement is Incorrect
Even if you got \(1000\) \(\text{Heads}\) in a row, but the probability of next toss to be \(\text{Tails}\) is still \(50\%\).

But why exactly is above statement False?
Because Law of Large Numbers says as \(n\to\infty\) our Sample Average \(\to\) True Average.
So even if we got \(1000\) \(\text{Heads}\) in a row, there is still \(\infty\) tosses are left to make our Sample Average a True Average.
