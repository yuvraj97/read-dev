In two sample test we have 2 samples \((\)say one sample is \(X_1,X_2,\cdots,X_n\) and another sample is \(Y_1,Y_2,\cdots,Y_m\)\()\)
Here one sample is generally used as a reference for another.

For example:
Say that we want to test the efficacy of a drug.
Now say you have some patients.
Then you make two groups of those patients.

  • One is a Treatment group here are the patients who are willing to go for a new drug, so we give them our drug and observe how the drug is behaving on the patient.
  • And another one is Control Group here are the patients who aren't willing to go for brand new drug So they will be given a placebo, placebo isn't any medicine, here we give patients a healthy environment, nutritious diet. and check that will they overcome the disease without any drug.

    • So we assume that there is a placebo effect and we want our drug to perform better then that.

    Now back to our example of R2F vs WilMA
    R2F WilMA
    Average(mins) \(\overline{X}_n=24.2\) \(\overline{Y}_m=15.9\)
    Std.Dev(mins) \(\sigma_X=2.1\) \(\sigma_Y=1.3\)
    Sample size \(n=72\) \(m=56\)
    We can see that \(\overline{X}_n \gt \overline{Y}_m\)
    So based on our sample can we say with certainty that WilMA is faster then R2F?
    No we cannot say it with certainty, there are fluctuations(variance) in our data.
    It might happened that by chance our sample responds well for WilMA.
    So just based on a single sample we can't say that WilMA is faster then R2F.
    So based on our sample and knowing that \(\overline{X}_n \gt \overline{Y}_m\) we cannot say that \(\mu_X \gt \mu_Y\)
    Now we are getting an intuition to take these fluctuation into account.


    YouTube Example

    According to     THIS(you don't need to read it) post among \(4,971\) randomly samples Americans , \(32\%\) report to get at least some of there news on YouTube. Can we conclude that at most \(1/3\) of all Americans get at least some of there news on YouTube?

    Remember: We have sample of \(4,971\) Americans and we want to say something for all the Americans, so whatever we deduce from this data will not be going to be \(100\%\) true.
    Modelling Assumptions
    We have \(n=4,971\) observations.
    So we have \(n\) i.i.d. r.v. and say they have a Bernoulli distribution.
    \(X_1,...,X_n\stackrel{iid}{\sim}\text{Ber}(p)\)
    Why Bernoulli distribution?
    Here our observations \(X_i\) is either \(0\) or \(1\).
    \(1\) corresponds to that the person did get at least some of his/her news on YouTube.
    \(0\) corresponds to that the person did not get at least some of his/her news on YouTube.
    and probability that the person get at least some of his/her news on YouTube is \(p\).
    so our statistical model is \( \left( \{0,1\}, (Ber(p))_{p \in (0,1)} \right) \)
    we have \(\overline{X}_n=0.32\)
    If it's indeed true that at most \(1/3\) of all Americans get at least some of there news on YouTube, then
    • Our true proportion \(p=1/3\)
    • \(\mathbb{E}[X_i]=p\)
    • variance: \(\text{var}(X_i) =\frac{p(1-p) }{n}\)
    • by CLT: \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.33}{0.33(1-0.33)}\sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\)
      • Now if we have a sample and we calculate \(\overline{X}_n\) then \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.33}{0.33(1-0.33)}\) should be a plausible number according to std. normal.
      Like if we got \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.33}{0.33(1-0.33)} = 100\) this is very unlikely for \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\) to take a value 100.
      This indicates that our assumption of \(p=1/3\) might not be correct.
    According to our data \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.33}{0.33(1-0.33)} =-1.5;\quad -1.5\) is a plausible number, but how much plausible we will see it later.

    Coin Toss Example

    Say you toss a coin \(50\) times and it land heads \(23\) times, can we conclude that that coin is significantly unfair?
    Modelling Assumptions
    We have \(n=50\) observations.
    So we have \(n\) i.i.d. r.v. and again we have a Bernoulli distribution.
    \(X_1,...,X_n\stackrel{iid}{\sim}\text{Ber}(p)\)
    Why Bernoulli distribution?
    Here our observations \(X_i\) is again either \(0\) or \(1\).
    \(1\) corresponds to that Heads.
    \(0\) corresponds to Tails.
    probability of getting a Head is \(p\).
    so our statistical model is again \( \left( \{0,1\}, (Ber(p))_{p \in (0,1)} \right) \)
    we have \(\overline{X}_n=23/50=0.46\)
    If it's true that our coin is fair\((p=0.5)\) then,
    • Our true proportion \(p=0.5\)
    • \(\mathbb{E}[X_i]=p\)
    • variance: \(\text{var}(X_i) =\frac{p(1-p) }{n}\)
    • by CLT: \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.5}{0.5(1-0.5)}\sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\)
      • Now if we have a sample and we calculate \(\overline{X}_n\) then \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.5}{0.5(1-0.5)}\) should be a plausible number according to std. normal.
    According to our data \(\sqrt{n}\frac{\overline{X}_n-0.5}{0.5(1-0.5)} =-0.56;\quad -0.56\) is very plausible number, but how much plausible we will see it later.
    What we did is first we assume that the coin is fair, then we fail to find a contradiction for the fairness of the coin.
    So we are not able to show that the coin is unfair.
    So we make a conclusion: "It is not unlikely that the coin is fair".
    We should not make conclusion like: "It is likely that the coin is fair".
