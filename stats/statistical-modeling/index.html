Why Statistical modeling?
Say we have \(X_1,X_2,....,X_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim } X\), and the goal of statistics is to learn the distribution of \(X\).

  • Now if we say that \(X\in \{0,1\}\).
    Now which model best describes \(X\)?
    \(X\in\{0,1\}\) and we have not much choices for that kind of r.v.
    So \(X\) has Bernoulli distribution, \(X\sim Ber(p)\), now we only have to learn about the parameter \(p\).

  • Now say we have \(n\) students, And the \(X_i\)'s are the number of siblings of each student (including him/her self).
    Let the dataset be like: 1,2,3,2,4,2,2,2,4,2,1,3,4,1,2,2,4,...... What we can do is, we can determine the proportion of \(x\) siblings, for individual \(x\)

    \[x\] \[1\] \[2\] \[3\] \[4\] \[5\] \[6\] \[\geq 7\]
    \[\mathbb{P}[X=x]\] \[p_1\] \[p_2\] \[p_3\] \[p_4\] \[p_5\] \[p_6\] \[\sum_{i\geq 7}p_i\]
    Here we have 7 parameters to learn (or say 6)
    Now which model best describes \(X\)?
    We have one distribution, with only one parameter, that can describe our random variable \(X\)
    And that's Poisson distribution, but Poisson takes values \(0,1,2,3,...\)
    And we have \(X\in \{1,2,3,...\}\).
    So rather then looking at \(X\) let's look at \(X-1\) and \(X-1 \sim \{0,1,2,...\} \)
    Now we can say \(X-1\sim Poiss(\lambda)\)
    Poisson distribution: \[\mathbb{P}(X=x)=\frac{\lambda^x}{x!}e^{- \lambda} \] Now we only have to learn this one parameter \(\lambda\)
So using Statistical modeling we can describe our r.v. with just one single mathematical formalization

Statistical model
Say an experiment has outcomes(sample) \(X_1,X_2,...,X_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim } X\), Sample space of \(X\) is denoted by \(E\) and the distribution of r.v. \(X\) is denoted by \(\mathbb{P}\). A Statistical Model associated with our statistical experiment is a pair \(\left(E,(\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}\right)\)
\(E\): Sample space
\((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}\): it's family of probability distribution on \(E\)
\(\Theta\): Parameter set

Parametric, nonparametric and semiparametric models
  • Usually, we will assume that statistical model is well specified that means \(\exists\theta \) such that \(\mathbb{P}=\mathbb{P}_\theta\)
    Now what is meant by well specified?
    Say we have an \(\infty\) dimensional sample set, a sample set of all probability distribution on the Real line.
    Here in the image shown below, we took all kinds of probability distribution(on Real line) like Bernoulli, Poisson, ...

    Somewhere in that \(\infty\) dimensional space, there is our true \(\mathbb{P}\) from which we get our data
    We took a tiny portion of that \(\infty\) dimensional space,\((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}\), with parameter \(\theta\in\Theta\), (For example: Family of Bernoulli distribution)
    And a model being well specified mean our true distribution \(\mathbb{P}\) is inside that tiny portion \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}\)
    And in a misspecified model \(\mathbb{P}\) is out of \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}\).
    And the \(\theta\) where our true distribution \((\mathbb{P})=\mathbb{P}_\theta\) is called true parameter which is unknown, and the goal of our statistical experiment is to estimate \(\theta\), get some confidence intervals and also check it's property like in our kiss example we are checking the property that "Do people have a preference of turning their head to the right when kissing?" there our parameter is \(p\) and we are checking the property that is \(p=1/2\) or \(p\neq 1/2\)
  • Remember that parameter space is different than the sample space, like for a Bernoulli r.v.(say \(X\)) the parameter is \(p\) and \(p\in (0,1)\) so parameter space is \((0,1)\), and \(X\in \{0,1\}\) so the sample space is \(\{0,1\}\)
    And we often assume that \(\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) for \(d \geq 1\)
    Example:
      \(Ber(p)\) is an example of \(\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^1\) where \(p\) is the only unknown parameter
      \(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma)\) is an example of \(\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\) where \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are two unknown parameters
    So when our statistical model is well specified means \(\exists\theta \) such that \(\mathbb{P}=\mathbb{P}_\theta\), where \(\theta\) is our true parameter and the parameter space \(\Theta \in \mathbb{R}^d;d\geq 1\) then the model is called Parametric
  • Sometime we could have \(\Theta\) to be \(\infty\) dimensional, so say we have a space of all of the probability distribution, so all of those probability distributions have there own parameters and there are \(\infty\) of them, these kind of models are called nonparametric
  • If our \(\Theta\) has two components \(\Theta=\Theta_1\times \Theta_2\) where \(\Theta_1\) is finite dimensional and \(\Theta_2\) is \(\infty\) dimensional So here we have two things to learn, one of then is finite dimensional and another one is \(\infty\) dimensional, these kinds of models are called semiparametric models.
    In these kinds of models, we only care to learn one of them, and mostly it's the parametric/finite dimensional one, and the \(\infty\) dimensional one is known as nuisance parameter.
    semiparametric models will not be covered here.
Examples for parametric models:
  1. For \(n\) Bernoulli trials
    See Answer
    \[\left( \{0,1\}, (Ber(p))_{p\in(0,1)} \right) \] So sample space \(E=\{0,1\}\)
    • Well for \(E\) we can say \(E=[0,1]\) or \([-1,2]\)
      But we should write the smallest possible sample space and that's \(\{0,1\}\)
    Family of probability distribution \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}=(Ber(p))_{p\in(0,1)}\)
    Parameter space \(\Theta=(0,1)\)
  2. \(X_1,X_2,...,X_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim } Poiss(\lambda)\) for some unknown \(\lambda \gt 0\)
    See Answer
    \[ \left( \mathbb{N},( Poiss(\lambda))_{\lambda \gt 0} \right) \] So sample space \(E=\mathbb{N}\)
    • We can say that sample space is \(\mathbb{R}\text{ or }\mathbb{Z}\text{ or }\mathbb{N}\)
      But the smallest possible sample space is \(\mathbb{N}\)
    Family of probability distribution \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}=( Poiss(\lambda)_{\lambda \gt 0})\)
    Parameter space \(\lambda=(0,\infty)\)
  3. \(X_1,X_2,...,X_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim } \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\) for some unknown \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(\sigma^2 \gt 0\)
    See Answer
    \[\left( \mathbb{R}, ( \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2))_{(\mu,\sigma^2)\in \mathbb{R} \times (0,\infty)} \right) \]
    So sample space \(E=\mathbb{R}\)
    Family of probability distribution \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}=( \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2))_{(\mu,\sigma^2)\in \mathbb{R} \times (0,\infty)}\)
    Parameter space \((\mu,\sigma^2)\in \mathbb{R} \times (0,\infty)\)
    • For parameter space, we have two unknown parameters to learn \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^2\)
      \(\mu\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(\sigma^2\in(0,\infty)\) So our \(2\)-Dimensional parameter space is \((\mu,\sigma^2)\in \mathbb{R} \times (0,\infty)\)
  4. \(X_1,X_2,...,X_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim } \mathcal{N_d}(\mu,I_d)\) for some unknown \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d\)
    Now here our observation \(X_1,...,X_n\) are i.i.d Gaussian random vector, \(X_i\in\mathbb{R}^d\)
    \(I_d\) is the covariance matrix it's not unknown it's the identity, it's not the parameter (We will came to it later)
    See Answer
    \[\left( \mathbb{R}^d, ( \mathcal{N_d}(\mu,I_d))_{(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d)} \right) \]
    So sample space \(E=\mathbb{R}^d\)
    Family of probability distribution \((\mathbb{P_\theta})_{\theta\in\Theta}=( \mathcal{N_d}(\mu,I_d))_{(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d)}\)
    Parameter space \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^d\)
Examples for nonparametric models:
Here in nonparametric case we put assumptions on the function that describes our random variable, that how is it looks like.
There are two functions that describes a continuous r.v.
  1. PDF:
    It can be any function that fulfill the properties of a PDF.
    Properties of PDF:
    • It's well integrable.
    • It Integrates to \(1\).
    • And it's non negative.
    Properties of function are essential for modeling.
    So it can be any PDF we can think of, commonly used shape is unimodal
    Unimodal PDF increases on \((-\infty,a)\) and decreases from \((a,\infty)\) , So example of it are Gaussian, Exponential, ... or any distribution with (smallest possible) sample space\((E)=\mathbb{R}\)
    Sample space \(E=\mathbb{R}\)
    Parameter space will be set of all unimodal PDF
    \(\mathbb{P}_\theta\) is distribution with unimodal PDF
  2. CDF:
    It can be any function that fulfill the properties of a CDF.
    Properties of CDF:
    • It's invertible
    • It's Range is \([0,1]\)
    Sample space \(E=[0,1]\)
    Parameter space: all invertible CDF
Extra examples:
  • Linear regression model:
    if \((X_1,Y_1),(X_2,Y_2),...,(X_n,Y_n)\in \mathbb{R}^d\times\mathbb{R}\) are i.i.d.
    Here \(X_i\)'s are the input and \(Y_i\)'s are the output, and we want to know how \(Y\in \mathbb{R}\) depends on \(X\in \mathbb{R}^d\)
    According to Linear regression model we assume linear dependent of \(X\) on \(Y\)
    \(X_i\)'s are \(d-\) dimensional random vectors:
    \[\vec{X_i}=\begin{pmatrix} X_i^{(1)}\\ X_i^{(2)}\\ \vdots \\ X_i^{(d)} \end{pmatrix} \quad,\quad \vec{\beta}=\begin{pmatrix} \beta_1\\ \beta_2\\ \vdots \\ \beta_d \end{pmatrix}\]
    And \(Y_i\) is linearly dependent on \(X_i\) So:
    \(Y_i=\beta_1X_i^{(1)}+\beta_2X_i^{(2)}+\dots +\beta_dX_i^{(d)}+\varepsilon_i\quad\) \(;\varepsilon_i\stackrel{iid}{\sim }\mathcal{N}(0,1)\)
    We can write it as dot product of two vectors:
    \[Y_i=(\beta_1\ \beta_2\ \cdots\ \beta_d) \begin{pmatrix} X_i^{(1)}\\ X_i^{(2)}\\ \vdots \\ X_i^{(d)} \end{pmatrix} + \varepsilon_i\ \\ \Rightarrow Y_i = \vec{\beta}^T\ \vec{X_i} + \varepsilon_i\]
    What is our sample space \(E?\)
    Our inputs are \(X_i\in\mathbb{R}^d\) and \(Y_i\in\mathbb{R}\)
    So our sample space is \(E=\mathbb{R}^d\times\mathbb{R}\)
    What is our parameter space \(\Theta?\)
    Here \(\vec{\beta}\in\mathbb{R}^d\) is the only unknown we want to learn
    So our parameter space \(\Theta\) is \(\Theta = \mathbb{R}^d\)
  • Cox proportional Hazard model:(example of semiparametric model)
    It was used in survival analysis, where we model the probability that people live longer after a time period \(t\)
    We have \((X_1,Y_1),(X_2,Y_2),...,(X_n,Y_n)\in \mathbb{R}^d\times\mathbb{R}\) and the conditional distribution of \(Y\) given \(X=x\) had CDF \(F\) of the form: \[ F(t)=1-\exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u)e^{(\vec{\beta})^T x}du\ \right) \] Survival function \(\mathbb{P}(X \gt t) = 1-F(t)\)
    So \(F(t)=1-\mathbb{P}(X \gt t)\), \(F(t)\) is the probability of not surviving after time \(t\)
    Now if we want to estimate \(F(t)\) there are two unknowns, one is \(h\) it's the non-negative nuisance function (that we don't care about)and another is \(\vec{\beta}\) its a vector.
    We don't need to estimate the whole \(F(t)\), we just have to estimate \(\vec{\beta}\in \mathbb{R}^d\), so that we can understand the influence of \(X\) on \(F(t)\)
    \(X\in \mathbb{R}^d\) \(X\) contains a lot's of information about the patients like their age, diet, ... And by estimating \(\vec{\beta}\) we can find out which of the information in \(X\) are impacting the survival positively, which of them are affecting it negatively, which of them are not relevant.
