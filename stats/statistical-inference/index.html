Goal of this chapter is to introduce us to this Trinity of statistical inference:

Estimation

Here we get a single number, to estimate the parameter of the distribution.
For example, in our kiss example the estimator of the proportion \(p\) of couples that turn there head to the right is \(\hat{p}=\overline{R}_n\) and according to our example \(\overline{R}_n=64.5\%\)
We knew that \(\overline{R}_n\) is an estimator of \(p\) but is \(64.5\%\) also an estimator of \(p?\)
Well, we think of estimators as random variables rather than some numbers.
Now the question arises:
Why random variables rather than numbers?
So the answer is because we want to assess the performance, we can't assess it's performance if it's just a number (it will be cleared later when we talk about confidence interval)

Confidence Intervals

Now we have our estimator we can now talk about our confidence intervals.
When we estimate our parameter, then there is some error associated with our estimator.
Confidence interval gives us an interval where our estimator might be present with some level of confidence.
Like say we estimate our estimator to be \(= 0.645\) and we say "\([0.56,0.73]\) is a \(95\%\) confidence interval for our estimator".
Notice that we have a confidence level of \(95\%\) So it might be possible if we perform the same experiment then our estimator falls outside of the confidence interval.
And also notice that each experiment has it's own confidence interval because they might have different values for the estimator (we will see it later when we talk about confidence interval)

Hypothesis testing

It's important if we want to make a discovery.
Using hypothesis testing we can make a statement like:
"We find statistical evidence that more couples turn their head to the right when kissing"
So here we are dealing with a YES or NO answer type questions.
Like "Yes, we find statistical evidence" or "No, we didn't find statistical evidence"


So this is the Trinity.
But before it, we need to have an understanding of statistical modeling
